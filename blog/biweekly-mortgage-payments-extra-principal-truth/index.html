Biweekly mortgage payments: what actually shortens the loan

By Daniel Morris Updated 2026-05-14 6 min read

Quick answer

Quick answer: True biweekly plans send 26 half-payments per year—the cash flow equivalent of 13 monthly payments instead of 12. The interest savings come from extra principal per calendar year, not from magic cadence. If your servicer holds partial payments in suspense until a full monthly bill arrives, you may get the budgeting rhythm without the interest benefit—read posting rules before you pay enrollment fees.

The arithmetic in one line

Assume a contractual monthly P&I of $2,000 ($24,000/year on 12 payments).

Cadence Cash to servicer / year Extra vs 12× monthly
12 full monthly payments $24,000 —
Biweekly halves ($1,000 each × 26) $26,000 +$2,000 principal acceleration (~one extra monthly)
DIY: monthly + 1/12 of a payment $24,000 + ~$167×12 ≈ $26,000 Same annual principal story without middlemen

So the “biweekly advantage” is mostly automation + one extra month of principal per year. Anything that reliably moves that much principal early produces a similar curve—see how extra mortgage payments reduce interest costs.

Third-party programs: fees vs bank bill-pay

If a processor charges $300–$500/year, weigh that against simply increasing your auto-pay by 1/12 of a payment monthly ($167 in the example above). Fee drag eats the first chunk of any benefit on smaller balances.

When to prioritize mortgage frequency at all

If revolving APRs are double digits, paying those first typically beats micro-optimizing mortgage cadence on a 6–7% first lien. Order of operations: match any employer 401(k) match, clear toxic debt, keep reserves, then accelerate the mortgage.

Frequently asked questions

Will biweekly payments always slash my term?

Only if extra principal is actually applied early each year. The effect scales with rate, balance, and how many true extra dollars you pay—not the envelope branding.

Is weekly better than biweekly?

Usually marginal unless the servicer credits partial payments immediately; verify posting before assuming weekly wins.

Does biweekly change my note rate?

No—it changes principal timing if funds post correctly.

Can I replicate biweekly with one annual lump sum?

Yes—roughly one extra monthly P&I applied once per year is in the same ballpark as the 13th-payment effect, though earlier monthly chunks often save slightly more interest than a single December lump.
