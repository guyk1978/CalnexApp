Refinance break-even: when closing costs actually pay back

Quick answer

Quick answer: Refinance “wins” on rate savings alone when you keep the new loan long enough for cumulative payment reductions (and removed MI, if any) to exceed after-tax-style cash you spent on lender and third-party costs—usually expressed as months to break even = net one-time costs ÷ monthly true savings. If you sell or refi again before that month count, you may never recover the fees even if the new payment feels better.

The break-even formula

Define:

  • Net costs = origination, appraisal, title, recording, discount points, prepaid interest days you would not have paid otherwise, minus lender credits. If you finance closing costs into the new balance, add the extra interest on that rolled amount to a “true cost” view (rough rule: financed costs lengthen break-even).
  • True monthly savings = (old P&I + old MI) − (new P&I + new MI) on the same amortization basis. Do not count escrow timing quirks as permanent savings.

Break-even months ≈ Net costs ÷ True monthly savings. If savings are $198/month and net costs are $6,500, break-even ≈ 6,500 ÷ 198 ≈ 33 months.

Worked example ($400k)

Illustrative rate/term refi on a $400,000 balance, 30-year amortization, comparing only P&I (taxes/insurance excluded).

Loan tag Note rate Monthly P&I
Current 7.00% ~$2,661
New offer 6.25% ~$2,463

Monthly P&I savings ≈ $198. With $6,500 net recoverable costs, break-even ≈ 33 months. Planned stay under three years? This refi is unlikely to pay back on interest savings alone unless another benefit (MI removal, debt consolidation, cash-out ROI) carries its own ledger.

Points change the math—you pay more upfront for a lower rate. See origination fees and discount points before you treat “no points” as cheaper.

When a lower payment still loses money

Restarting a fresh 30-year amort clock after you have already paid down years on the old loan cuts P&I sharply while often raising lifetime interest versus staying on the old amortization schedule. Break-even on monthly cash flow can still occur, but wealth (equity + interest paid) may lag—model both “payment breakeven” and “total interest paid” in the mortgage calculator.

If you refi for payment relief but want to avoid re-extending interest, keep the same or shorter remaining term or commit to systematic extra principal—how extra mortgage payments reduce interest costs.

Cash-out and blended goals

Cash-out raises balance; break-even on “rate drop” still applies to the non-cash-out portion, while proceeds need their own return story (paying off 24% APR cards is a high hurdle rate; remodeling is not). Compare lien choices in home equity loan vs HELOC vs cash-out refinance.

Recast (same note, lower balance payment) is a different lever than refi—see mortgage recast vs refinance when you have a lump sum but like your current rate.

Checklist before you lock

  1. Normalize MI in old vs new—if PMI drops via appraisal, split that savings from pure rate savings.
  2. Build two break-evens: cash-paid costs vs financed costs + interest on financed piece.
  3. Stress escrow: tax/insurance can move independently of rate—do not attribute reassessment to “refi savings.”
  4. Write your expected hold months; if hold < break-even, you need a non-rate reason (cash-out ROI, MI removal, divorce buyout timing).

Frequently asked questions

Is refinancing worth it if my rate only drops 1%?

Sometimes—divide your net costs by monthly true savings. On large balances, a 1% move can clear fees quickly; on small balances or short holds, it may not. Use the $400k example above as a template.

How long does it take to break even on refinancing?

There is no universal timeline; it is purely costs divided by verified monthly savings. Typical refi cost bundles often land in the ~24–60 month range for purely rate-driven stories—your quote dictates the truth.

Should I roll closing costs into the new loan?

It preserves cash today but raises balance and interest paid on those dollars—break-even lengthens unless you invest the cash at a higher after-tax return than the mortgage rate.

Does skipping a payment at closing mean I saved money?

Usually that is accrual timing, not economic savings—confirm with your closing disclosure and amortization schedule.
