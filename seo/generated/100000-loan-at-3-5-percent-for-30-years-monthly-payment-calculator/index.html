Is a 30-year loan worth it at 3.5%?

Worth it when $449.04 fits net cash flow after reserves—not when it maxes out DTI. You trade $265.84/mo versus a 15-year schedule for about $32,977.23 less lifetime interest.

Scenario lens: shorter horizon trade-offs

Stretching the same $100,000.00 to 15 years at 3.5% drops the installment to about $714.88 but adds roughly $32,977.23 in lifetime interest versus this 30-year note.

At 5% instead of 3.5%, expect about $536.82 per month and $93,255.78 total interest.

Refinancing and affordability

Refi only if break-even months fit your hold period (refinance break-even). Affordability includes escrows and other debts—see DTI qualification.

Who this fits

  • Stable income households prioritizing total interest over minimum payment.
  • Borrowers with a verified plan to stay past break-even after a refi.

Pause if emergency savings are thin or high-APR unsecured debt should clear first.

Risks of a shorter amortization

Main risk is liquidity: extra principal is hard to access without borrowing again. Confirm prepayment rules on your note.

First-time buyer stretch: Underwriting may approve a 15-year payment near $714.88, but you choose 30 years at $449.04 to cap lifetime interest near $61,656.09.

Frequently asked questions

What is the monthly payment on $100,000.00 at 3.5% for 30 years?

About $449.04 per month in principal and interest for 360 on-time payments (total repayment about $161,656.09).

How much total interest will I pay?

Roughly $61,656.09 over the full term if you do not prepay.

How does 30 years compare with 15 years at the same rate?

At 3.5%, the 15-year payment is about $714.88 versus $449.04 on 30 years—a $265.84 monthly gap and about $32,977.23 difference in scheduled interest if both run full term.

What if I add $100.00 extra toward principal each month?

Payoff lands near month 261 with about $42,827.94 in interest—savings versus minimum pay depend on servicer application rules.
